Assumptions and interpretation

  • Laser supply (5 V): schematic states 5 V, 600 mA for laser. The board has two laser channels (Channel 1 and Channel 2). Assumption A1: the 600 mA figure is per laser channel. If instead 600 mA is total, scale results by 0.5.
  • ±15 V rails: schematic lists 300 mA on +15 V and 150 mA on -15 V (used by op amps). Treat those as the stated needs (nominal). A 20% margin is applied for a conservative "max" number unless a component max is explicitly stated.
  • TEC H-bridge and H-bridge rails (+8 V / -8 V): the TEC can be driven from the H-bridge with TEC current range -1.5 A to +2.5 A and a maximum TEC voltage of ±3.8 V. The H-bridge power rails are ±8 V (LM7808/LM7908). We compute delivered TEC power using the stated TEC voltage/current limits (3.8 V × I). For rail power we show a conservative worst-case where both TEC channels draw maximum current in the same direction (worst-case rail current).
  • Control supplies ±6 V: LT3045/LT3094 provide ±6 V control supplies for gate drivers and analog circuits. The schematic does not list explicit currents; we assume small control currents for nominal operation and a higher conservative max for worst-case. These are flagged clearly below.
  • 3.3 V MCU rail: Teensy and digital peripherals. If you have measured MCU current, replace the assumed values. We use 200 mA nominal and 500 mA worst-case (including peripherals, level shifters, SPI/DDS activity).
  • Margins: where the schematic gives a single number we treat it as nominal and add a 20% margin to produce a conservative "max" unless a component maximum is explicitly given.
  • Two scenarios reported: (A) Nominal (typical expected operation) and (B) Conservative worst-case (both lasers on, both TECs at maximum drive, control rails at high load). Use the worst-case numbers for power supply selection and thermal design.

Recommendations (practical)

  1. Power supplies: size the ±8 V H-bridge rails to handle the worst-case rail current (design example: 8 V, 5 A capability per rail) and provide adequate thermal dissipation for MOSFET losses. If you expect both TECs to never be at full same-direction current simultaneously, you can reduce rail current accordingly — but design for the worst-case if reliability is critical.
  2. 5 V supply: choose a 5 V regulator capable of at least 2.0 A if you want comfortable margin for two lasers (1.2 A) plus other 5 V loads and inrush. A 5 V @ 2 A supply gives headroom and reduces regulator stress.
  3. ±15 V rails: the quoted currents are modest; a ±15 V supply rated for 500 mA on +15 V and 300 mA on -15 V (or a single supply with split rails) gives margin for future additions.
  4. 3.3 V MCU: a 3.3 V regulator rated for 1 A gives comfortable margin for Teensy, DDS, and level-shifter loads.
  5. Thermal design: the H-bridge MOSFETs and TECs will generate significant heat at high currents — ensure heatsinking and board copper are sized for the expected dissipation (use the rail-power numbers for worst-case thermal budget).
  6. Measurement & verification: measure actual currents on each rail during representative operation (laser on, TEC heating/cooling, DDS active) and update the budget with measured values before finalizing supply selection.

How to refine these numbers

To tighten the budget and avoid overdesign, provide or measure:

  • Actual laser current draw per channel under your operating conditions (is 0.6 A per laser continuous?).
  • Expected TEC operating currents during normal operation (not worst-case). If TECs rarely run at ±2.5 A simultaneously, you can reduce rail sizing.
  • Measured current for Teensy + peripherals and for the DAC / DDS when active.
  • MOSFET Rds(on) and expected conduction losses at the chosen gate drive voltages — this lets you compute rail-to-load efficiency and actual rail dissipation (not just delivered TEC power).
